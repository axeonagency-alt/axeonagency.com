Why We Only Hire T-Shaped Creatives

A look inside Axeon's hiring philosophy and team structure.

Axeon Team Agency Life 6 min read

Every agency claims to hire "the best people." It's a meaningless line until you define what "best" actually means for the work you do. At Axeon, it means one specific thing: we hire T-shaped creatives, and we don't make exceptions for this.

If you haven't come across the term, a T-shaped person has broad, working knowledge across many disciplines — the horizontal bar of the T — and deep, genuine expertise in one — the vertical stroke. Not a jack-of-all-trades. Not a narrow specialist either. Both, deliberately combined.

Here's why that shape matters more than any resume line item.

Specialists alone create silos

A brilliant designer who doesn't understand how development constraints work will hand off files that break in the browser. A strong developer who doesn't understand design intent will "fix" things in ways that quietly kill the point of the design. Put five narrow specialists in a room and you don't get five times the output — you get five people talking past each other and a project manager translating between them.

Depth without breadth creates friction at every handoff. And in an agency, handoffs happen constantly.

Generalists alone lack the edge that wins work

The opposite failure mode is just as real. Someone who's "pretty good" at everything and truly excellent at nothing produces work that's competent but forgettable. Clients don't come to an agency for competent. They come for the thing that makes a competitor's team look average.

That edge only comes from real depth — someone who has spent years obsessing over one craft, not someone who dabbled in it between other responsibilities.

We're not looking for people who can do everything a little. We're looking for people who are excellent at one thing and genuinely curious about the rest.

What this looks like on our team

Our designers understand enough front-end development to know what's realistic to build, and they design with that in mind — no jarring gap between the mockup and the shipped product. Our developers have enough design sense to catch a spacing issue or a weak hierarchy before a client ever sees it. Our strategists understand enough about both to write briefs that don't fall apart the moment they hit production.

Nobody on the team is limited to their lane, but everybody has a lane they own completely.

Why this shows up in the hiring process

When we interview, we're not just testing for portfolio quality in someone's core discipline. We're looking for signs of range:

  • Can they explain their design decisions in terms of business impact, not just aesthetics?
  • Can a developer talk through a design choice and explain why it works, not just how to build it?
  • Have they taught themselves something outside their core skill because it made them better at their actual job?
  • Do they ask questions that cross disciplines during a project walkthrough, or only questions inside their own lane?

Depth is easy to verify — a portfolio does most of that work. Breadth is harder to fake, and it's usually the better predictor of whether someone will thrive on a small, fast-moving team.

The result: fewer people, better output

This hiring bar is a big part of why Axeon runs lean. A team of T-shaped people needs fewer handoffs, fewer meetings to translate between departments, and fewer people in the room to make a good decision. Everyone can speak enough of everyone else's language to move fast without breaking things.

It also means every project gets people who care about the whole outcome, not just their individual piece of it. That's not a personality trait we hope for after hiring — it's a structural result of who we choose to hire in the first place.
